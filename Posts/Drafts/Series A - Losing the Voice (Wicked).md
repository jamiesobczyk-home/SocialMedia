I saw Wicked for the fifth time last night, and this time the character that stuck with me wasn't Elphaba. It was the professor who slowly loses his voice.

Dr. Dillamond is the one who actually knows things. As the regime tightens, he gets talked over, pushed out, and eventually loses his voice entirely. By the time anyone wants the truth, the person who had it is gone.

I've watched smaller versions of that play out at work. A change review where the most experienced engineer flagged a risk, got waved off because the timeline mattered more, and then watched the exact failure they warned about take us down a week later. The warning was there. We just didn't make room for it.

The hard part is that silencing an expert rarely looks like silencing. It looks like moving fast. It looks like "we've already decided." It looks like the quietest person in the room being the one who knew the most, and never getting asked.

So now I watch for the voice that's going quiet. The engineer who stopped pushing back. The one who used to raise concerns and doesn't anymore. That isn't agreement. That's a warning I taught them to stop giving.

The expertise is usually still in the room. The question is whether anyone can still hear it.

When was the last time the person who knew the most was the person who said the least?
