I was smoothing a board with a hand plane. Long, clean shavings, the surface coming up like glass. Then I crossed into a new section and the plane dug in and tore a chunk out of the face.

Nothing about my grip or the blade had changed. The wood had. The grain ran one direction on most of the board and reversed in that one patch. Planing into it instead of with it, the same stroke that left glass tore the surface apart.

The fix wasn't pushing harder or buying a better plane. It was reading the grain and working with it. Plane the other direction on that patch. Lighten the cut. Switch to a scraper where the grain fought back.

I think about that every time I roll out the same process for everyone. The onboarding plan that worked beautifully for one hire confuses the next. The direct feedback one teammate appreciates shuts another one down. The technique was never the problem. I just wasn't reading the grain in front of me.

A good craftsman has one set of tools and many ways to use them. The wood tells you which one. People and teams do too, if you slow down enough to read them first.

Where have you forced one approach when the situation in front of you was asking for a different one?
