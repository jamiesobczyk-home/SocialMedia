I was working in my workshop on building a new project. The wood was expensive. Walnut, figured, exactly the right width for the piece. I had the fence set, the blade at the right angle, and I knew the cut was right.

Before I pushed it through, I grabbed a board out my scrap pile and ran that first.

The fence was off by just enough. A sixteenth of an inch. Nothing you'd catch by looking. Everything you'd notice in the finished piece.

I adjusted, ran another scrap, confirmed it, then cut the real board.

Test cuts aren't about doubt. They're about the gap between "looks right" and "is right." Those two things feel identical right up until they aren't.

I think about that before significant changes at work. A staging environment, a proof of concept, a tabletop walkthrough before a cutover. Not because I don't trust the plan. Because the plan always looks right from the planning side.

Here's the part I've had to learn the hard way: I'm most tempted to skip the test cut when I'm most confident. That's backwards. Confidence is when the cost of skipping feels lowest and the actual risk is highest. You're moving fast, you feel sure, and the scrap pile is right there.

Run the scrap first.

What's your version of the test cut before you commit to the real material?
