I was fitting a drawer in the shop a few weeks ago. One corner had a slight gap, so I trimmed the side down to close it.

It closed. But now the opposite corner didn't sit right. So I adjusted that. Then the drawer face was off. Then the reveal was uneven.

I spent an hour fixing a problem I had already fixed.

The mistake wasn't the first adjustment. The mistake was thinking the first adjustment only affected one thing.

A drawer isn't a single piece. It's a set of relationships. Change one dimension and you've changed all of them, whether you meant to or not. The experienced woodworker knows to stop, look at the whole thing, and understand what's actually connected before reaching for the saw.

IT systems work the same way. A quick fix to one component shifts load somewhere else. A configuration change resolves the alert and creates a new one three layers down. A process improvement in one team creates a bottleneck in another.

The problem usually isn't that people make changes. It's that they make changes while only looking at one piece.

Before you trim something down, know what it's connected to.
