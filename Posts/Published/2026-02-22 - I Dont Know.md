We lost a few hours last week because no one wanted to say "I don't know."

A ticket came in about slowness in one of our office networks. The engineer who picked it up ran through the standard checks and then spent an hour poking around, unsuccessfully, trying to find the root cause. They put the ticket on hold to get back to it after lunch.

While they were out, a second engineer took a look. After another hour they threw up their hands and asked a senior engineer for help.

The senior engineer pointed out a misconfiguration that wasn't obvious unless you knew what to look for.

The fix took five minutes once it was pointed out.

I get it. No one wants to be the person who doesn't have the answer. Especially when you're newer or when the team is busy. But "I don't know" followed by "let me pull someone in" is faster than pretending you do.

What we changed is small. If a ticket sits with you for more than 30 minutes without progress, you say something. You don't have to solve it. You just have to stop it from sitting there.

It's not about knowing everything. It's about not letting a problem age while you figure it out alone.

How does your team make it safe to say "I need another set of eyes on this"?
