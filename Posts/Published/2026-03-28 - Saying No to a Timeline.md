I had to tell a department head last month that we couldn't deliver what they were asking for on the timeline they wanted.

They needed a new site stood up with full redundancy in three weeks. The timeline was tied to a lease signing and they'd already committed to the date with their leadership.

We could have said yes. We could have cut corners, skipped testing, and pushed something out the door. But it wouldn't have been stable, and when it broke, we'd own that too.

So I said no. Not to the project, but to the timeline. I explained what we could deliver in three weeks and what we'd need another month for if they wanted it done right.

It was uncomfortable. They weren't happy. But they adjusted the lease timeline and we delivered something that actually worked.

Saying no up front and being clear about what we could actually deliver took pressure off the team. They had a timeline they could meet instead of one they were set up to miss.

I've learned this the hard way. Saying yes when you should say no doesn't make you helpful. It makes you the person who overpromises and underdelivers.

What we try to do now is be clear about what's possible and what's not before we commit. It's easier to negotiate a realistic timeline up front than to explain why you missed an unrealistic one later.

Saying no doesn't mean being a blocker. It means being honest about what you can actually deliver.

How does your team handle requests that aren't realistic without just becoming the team that always says no?
