When I first became a manager, I thought my reputation would come from how well I could present to leadership or how quickly I made decisions during incidents. I've since learned something much more important: your reputation is tied directly to your team's success.

If your team is thriving — projects finishing smoothly, incidents handled quickly, and people growing in their careers — everyone notices. If they're struggling, people notice that too.

That's why I've shifted my focus from "What am I achieving?" to "How do I create conditions where my team succeeds?" That means clearing the roadblocks that get in their way, making sure they have tools that actually help rather than just add noise, and giving them trust and ownership instead of micromanagement.

When they succeed, I succeed. When they grow, I grow. And when they feel supported, they produce results that speak louder than anything I could ever put on a resume.

The truth is, your reputation isn't built in meetings. It's built in the day-to-day wins of the people you lead.
