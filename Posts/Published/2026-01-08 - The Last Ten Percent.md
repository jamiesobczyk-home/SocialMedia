In woodworking, the last 10% is the part that tests your patience.

The project is assembled. It "works." It even looks pretty good from a few feet away.

But then comes the sanding. Breaking the edges. Applying the finish. Those little touch-ups you only notice when you slow down and really look.

That's the part that turns the piece into something you're proud to put your name on.

Work is a lot like that.

In IT, we often celebrate the moment the system is back up, the change is complete, or the new service is live. And we should. That's real progress.

But that last 10% is where reliability lives. Documentation that makes sense to someone who wasn't in the room. Monitoring that catches the problem before a user does. Clean handoffs so the next person isn't guessing. A quick post-incident note that helps future us.

It's not glamorous work. It doesn't always show up on status reports. And it's the first thing to get squeezed when everyone's busy.

But when we skip it, we pay for it later. Usually at the worst possible time.

I'm trying to get better at treating finish work as part of the job, not extra credit.

Where does the "last 10%" show up on your team, and what helps you protect time for it?
