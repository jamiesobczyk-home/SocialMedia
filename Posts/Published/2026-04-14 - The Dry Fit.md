I cut every piece for a small cabinet last weekend. Drew up the cut list, measured twice, ran everything through the saw. Felt good about it.

When I went to dry fit the drawers, nothing lined up. I stood there for a minute just staring at it. Went back to the cut list and found it immediately. I had written down the final dimension for the drawer sides but never made that last cut. The pieces were close, just not right.

So I pulled the tools back out and took them down to size. Twenty minutes of work I hadn't planned on.

The thing is, I caught it during a dry fit. No glue, no fasteners, nothing permanent. The mistake was real, but the cost was low.

That's what a dry fit is for. Not to confirm everything went perfectly. To find out what didn't before it's too late to fix it.

I think about that in IT work too. A staging environment, a change window test, a tabletop walkthrough before a cutover. These aren't extra steps. They're the part of the process that makes the rest of it recoverable.

A plan that looks complete on paper still has gaps. The dry fit is how you find them.
