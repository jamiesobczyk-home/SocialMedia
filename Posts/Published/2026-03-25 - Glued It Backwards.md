The cuts were perfect. The glue held. And I still had to take the whole thing apart because I wasn't paying attention when I assembled it.

I was gluing leg supports and had everything laid out. The cuts were right, the sanding was done, and I was ready to assemble. I worked through each joint carefully, clamped everything tight, and let it cure.

When I slid the two pieces together, one set of legs pointed down. The other set pointed up.

I'd glued one of the braces on backwards. Not because I didn't know which way it should go. Because I wasn't paying attention when it mattered. The wood was fine. The joinery was solid. But I built it wrong because I was on autopilot.

I see this happen in IT more than I want to admit. We provision a new account and copy the wrong template. We configure a device and flip a setting without reading what it actually does. We run a script in production that we meant to test in staging first.

The steps are right. The process is clear. But we're moving fast or distracted or both, and we don't catch the mistake until it's already done.

I still do this. The work feels routine so your brain checks out, and that's exactly when you make the mistake that costs you time.

The habit I'm trying to build is slowing down for two seconds before the irreversible step. Glue, deploy, delete, send. Just long enough to confirm I'm doing what I think I'm doing.

What does your team do to catch mistakes that happen when the work feels routine?
