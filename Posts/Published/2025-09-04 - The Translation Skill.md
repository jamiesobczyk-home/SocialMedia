When people think of IT expertise, they picture deep technical skills: routing protocols, security policies, scripting automation.

Those matter, but the skill I think is most underestimated? Translation.

The ability to take complex technical concepts and explain them clearly to someone without a technical background is huge. It's how you get buy-in for projects, avoid misunderstandings, and ensure stakeholders trust your recommendations.

I've seen technically brilliant engineers struggle because they couldn't communicate their ideas outside of their peer group. I've also seen people with solid — but not extraordinary — technical skills become invaluable because they could bridge that communication gap.

Translation is about more than just simplifying. It's about making the information relevant to the listener. An exec doesn't need a deep dive into packet flow; they need to understand the business impact. A front line employee doesn't need a security policy's legal text; they need to know how it affects their day-to-day.

If you want to stand out in IT, sharpen your communication skills. The better you translate, the more people will listen.
