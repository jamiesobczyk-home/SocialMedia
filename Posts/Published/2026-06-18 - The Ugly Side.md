I was building a shelf, surfacing rough boards down to clean stock. On one of them, I missed a side. I didn't see it until my tools were already put away.

Fixing it meant setting everything back up for ten minutes of work. I figured I'd catch it later in the process.

I never did.

The shelf is finished now, and it has an ugly side. One face I left rough because stopping felt like more trouble than it was worth at the time. I see it every time I walk past.

"Close enough" and "I'll fix it later" are the same trap. Both let you keep your momentum today by borrowing against a version of you that supposedly has more time tomorrow. That person rarely shows up.

We hit this on a hardware refresh recently. We were replacing every access point in a building before the students came back, and we finished the first floor before anyone noticed the new labels followed the old naming standard, not our current one. My first instinct was the shop instinct. Fix it later. The deadline's tight, keep moving.

One of my teammates pushed back. He pointed out how many "we'll fix it later" items we're already carrying, the ones we never find time for. He was right. The tight deadline today just gets replaced by a tighter one tomorrow.

The ten minutes up front is almost always cheaper than living with the rough side forever.

Where does "close enough" quietly become permanent on your team?
