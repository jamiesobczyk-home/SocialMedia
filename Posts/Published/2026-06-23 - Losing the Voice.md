I saw Wicked on stage for the fifth time last week, and this time the character that stuck with me wasn't Elphaba. It was the professor who slowly loses his voice.

Dr. Dillamond is the one who actually knows things. As the regime tightens, he gets talked over, pushed out, and eventually loses his voice entirely. By the time anyone wants the truth, the person who had it is gone.

I've watched smaller versions of that play out at work more often than I'd like. The hard part is that silencing an expert almost never looks like silencing. It looks like moving fast. It looks like "we've already decided." It looks like the most experienced person in the room being the one who says the least, and nobody noticing they stopped talking.

That's the part I missed for years. I kept watching for the loud disagreement or the shake of the head. The real signal was quieter than that. The engineer who used to push back and doesn't anymore. The one who used to flag the risk and now just builds what they're told. That isn't agreement. That's a warning I trained them to stop giving.

So now I pay attention to the voice that's going quiet, because by the time it goes silent the expertise has usually already walked out the door. I ask for the disagreements. I tell my team I value their questions.

The knowledge is almost always still in the room. The question is whether anyone can still hear it.

When was the last time the person who knew the most was the person who said the least?
