I built two pieces of furniture around the same time. One for me, one for my daughter.

The one for me turned out well. There's a joint I wasn't completely happy with but I let it go. I look at it sometimes and think it could've been better. Good enough though. It was just for me.

The one for my daughter was different. I double checked every cut. Made sure nothing shifted during the glue up. Sanded everything smooth. Watched the finish dry. Fixed anything that didn't look right before moving on.

Same tools. Same shop. Very different standard.

I didn't make a conscious decision to care more about one than the other. It just happened. Knowing she'd have it in her own home someday, that she'd use it, that it came from me — that changed how I worked.

I think about that in IT too. It's easy to let "good enough" become the default when you're not thinking about the person on the other end. The user who can't do their job because the system is slow. The team member who inherited a process nobody documented properly. The customer who just wants the thing to work.

Knowing who something is for has a way of raising your standard without you even having to think about it.

Who are you building for right now?
