A small thing that's helped my team more than I expected is getting specific about what "done" means.

Because in IT, "done" can mean a lot of things.

The system is back up. The change went in. The ticket stopped screaming.

And when you're busy, it's really tempting to close it out and move on. I get it. There is always another thing waiting.

But I've learned that "done enough" is how you create future work for yourself.

So we started asking a simple question before we call something finished:

Are we done, or did we just stop?

I've seen too many tickets where the fix worked, but nobody wrote down what they found. Two weeks later it happens again and we're back to rediscovering the same thing.

For us, "done" usually includes a few basics. We double-check the outcome, not just that the change applied. We leave decent notes in the ticket, so the next person isn't stuck guessing. We sanity-check monitoring and alerts, because changes have a way of creating new blind spots. We update the documentation with what we found and what we changed, while it's still fresh in our heads.

None of this is hard. It's just the part you want to skip when the issue is finally quiet. That's exactly why we try to do it anyway.

Because if we skip this part, we don't really save time. We just push the cost into the future, and it usually shows up as an escalation or a late night problem.

I'm trying to treat "definition of done" as a way to protect the team, not as extra bureaucracy.

What's something you wish your team did more consistently before calling work "done"?
