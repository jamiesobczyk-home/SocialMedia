Here's a pattern I've seen play out more than once.

An Architect designs something: a network component, an application structure, a process. It gets handed to Engineers to implement. Then Operations picks it up and supports it day to day.

And that works fine. Until something changes.

Maybe a problem surfaces during implementation. Maybe a new solution comes in and the old one has to adapt. Either way, something in the system is different now than what the original documentation described.

So who updates the docs?

The quick answer is everyone. Which in practice means no one.

The change gets made. People get busy. The documentation sits there, a record of how things used to work. The next person who needs it doesn't know that yet.

It's not that people don't care. It's just what you're currently working on always feels more important than updating something you're not actively using. So it gets assumed. Someone else will get to it. They don't.

What I've started doing with my teams is getting ahead of it. Who owns this application? This process? What does that ownership actually mean day to day?

Put a name next to it. One name. Not a team, not a role. A person.

Assumed ownership isn't ownership. It's just a gap waiting to be found.

What do you do to keep ownership from falling through the cracks?
