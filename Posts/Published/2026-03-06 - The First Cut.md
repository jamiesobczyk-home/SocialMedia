The first cut on a table saw is the one that matters most.

Not because it's the hardest. Because it sets the reference for every cut that follows.

I was ripping boards for a bookshelf a few weeks ago and rushed the first pass. The fence was close enough, the blade looked square, and I wanted to get moving. Three boards in I noticed they weren't lining up right. I checked the fence. It was off by an eighth of an inch.

Every board I'd cut was wrong by the same amount. I had to start over.

I think about that when we're setting up new systems or processes at work.

The first user you provision sets the pattern for how accounts get created. The first ticket template becomes the standard everyone copies. The first time you let a workaround slide, it becomes the way things are done.

If the reference is off at the start, everything that follows will be off too. And you won't notice until you're three boards in and nothing lines up.

I still rush this sometimes. It's hard to slow down at the beginning when you just want to get things moving.

The habit I'm building is checking the setup twice before I make the first cut. It takes an extra two minutes. It saves hours later.

What's one place where your team sets the reference, and how do you make sure it's right from the start?
