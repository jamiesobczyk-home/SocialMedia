I watched my son's track meet last week and kept thinking about how brave the kids running hurdles were.

Aren't they afraid of falling? Then one of them caught his toe on a hurdle and went down hard. But instead of sprawling out, he tucked into a forward roll and came up on his feet, barely breaking stride.

He wasn't unafraid of falling. He'd learned how to fall.

That stuck with me because it's the same thing we try to do with systems.

All systems fail eventually. The question isn't if, it's when and how. A server goes down. A circuit drops. A patch breaks something you didn't expect it to break.

The teams that handle it well aren't the ones who never fall. They're the ones who've thought through how they'll fall and what they'll do when it happens.

We spend a lot of time designing systems to stay up. But we don't always spend enough time designing how they'll fail. Where the dependencies are. What breaks first. What stays online when something else goes down.

What we try to do now is ask one extra question during design. When this fails, what happens next? Not if. When.

Because knowing how to fall is just as important as knowing how to run.

How does your team design for failure, not just uptime?
