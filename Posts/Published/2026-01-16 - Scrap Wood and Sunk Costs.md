In my shop, I have a bin of scrap wood that I keep "just in case."

And every so often I have to be honest with myself.

Some of it isn't scrap. It's clutter.

It has potential. It's a good species. It's not that small. And I can absolutely imagine the project I might use it for someday.

But if I haven't touched it in a year… I'm probably not going to.

Keeping it doesn't make me more prepared. It just makes it harder to find the pieces I actually use.

We had a similar moment at work recently.

We made the call to eliminate one of our monitoring applications. It had a lot of potential, but it was expensive, and we just never made the time to use it the way it deserved. On top of that, many of the features were already being handled by other tools.

So we asked the uncomfortable question: are we keeping this because it's helping us… or because we feel bad letting go of something with "potential"?

Letting it go wasn't a failure. It was a choice to simplify, reduce overlap, and focus on what we're actually going to maintain and use well.

Sometimes the most responsible thing you can do is stop carrying the things you're not going to invest in.

What have you "kept around" because it had potential — and what helped you finally decide to either commit to it or let it go?
