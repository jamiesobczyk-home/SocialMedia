The first thing I do when I start with a new team is listen for some time before I change anything.

I took over a network team a few years ago and the instinct was strong to start fixing things right away. There were obvious gaps. Processes that didn't make sense. Tools that seemed outdated.

But I didn't know the context yet. I didn't know why things were the way they were or what had already been tried.

So I spent the first few weeks in one-on-ones and sitting in on their daily work. I asked questions. I watched how they handled tickets, how they communicated during issues, what frustrated them.

By the end of that time I had a much clearer picture. Some of the things I thought were problems were actually workarounds for constraints I didn't know existed. And some of the real problems weren't the ones I would have guessed.

The changes we made after that landed better because the team knew I'd listened first.

I still have to remind myself of this. When you're new or when you're trying to prove yourself, the pressure to move fast is real. But rushing past the listening part usually means you fix the wrong things.

The habit I keep coming back to is simple. Some time asking questions before jumping to answers.

When you join a new team, how do you figure out what actually needs to change versus what just looks broken from the outside?
