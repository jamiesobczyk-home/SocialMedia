One of the biggest changes I've made as a manager isn't about how we troubleshoot.

It's about how we communicate while we troubleshoot.

Earlier in my career, I thought updates had to include the answer. If I didn't have the fix yet, I felt like I didn't have anything useful to say.

Now I think the most helpful updates are often the simplest. Here's what we know. Here's what we've tried. Here's what we're doing next. Here's when you'll hear from us again.

That's it.

It doesn't solve the problem by itself — but it reduces anxiety. It builds trust. And it gives the team room to work instead of constantly switching contexts to respond to pings.

I've learned that when people ask for updates, they're usually not trying to be difficult. They're trying to understand risk and make decisions. A steady cadence helps everyone breathe.

What does a "good update" look like on your team when you're still in the middle of it?
