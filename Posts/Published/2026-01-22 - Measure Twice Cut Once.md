In woodworking, "measure twice, cut once" sounds simple.

It's also the hardest advice to follow when you're excited to make progress.

Because measuring feels like you're not building anything. It feels slow. It feels like you're delaying the real work.

I can't tell you how many times I've skipped that step, confident in my measuring and my memory, only to find out during assembly that a piece was too long, or worse, too short.

Now I'm irritated. My flow is gone. And I'm much more likely to make another mistake because I'm trying to fix the first one while I'm still feeling it.

Work has a version of that too.

In IT, especially during busy weeks, it's tempting to rush a change because the solution feels obvious. You want to get it done and move on.

But I've learned that a little "measure twice" time saves a lot of "cut twice" pain.

For us, that looks like a quick peer review, even when it feels unnecessary. A clear rollback plan, even when you "won't need it." Confirming dependencies before the change window starts. Writing the stakeholder update before you hit deploy, so you're not scrambling mid-change.

None of that is complicated. It's just discipline.

And it's one of the best ways I know to protect the team's time and confidence.

What's your version of "measure twice" at work?
