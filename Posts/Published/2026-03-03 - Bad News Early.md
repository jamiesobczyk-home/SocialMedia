The carrier delayed the circuit by three weeks. We delayed telling anyone by two. Guess which one caused more problems.

We had a new office coming online and the carrier pushed the install date back three weeks. Not ideal, but it happens. Circuits get delayed. Permitting runs long. Someone misses a window.

The real issue was that we didn't tell the business unit until five days before the original go-live date. They had people scheduled to move in, equipment ordered, onboarding planned. And suddenly they had to scramble to set everyone up for WFH while we waited for the line to get installed.

The delay cost us three weeks. The late communication cost us trust.

I've learned this the hard way. When something slips, the instinct is to wait until you have a fix before you say anything. You don't want to sound the alarm if it might still work out. But that leaves everyone else without time to adapt.

What we do now is simple. If a timeline is at risk, we say it as soon as we know. Even if we don't have the solution yet. Even if it might still land on time.

Bad news early is better than bad news late. The business can work with three weeks of notice. They can't work with five days.

How does your team decide when to escalate a delay versus when to wait and see if you can fix it first?
