A sharp chisel does exactly what you ask it to do. That's the problem.

I was trimming a mortise last weekend, moving fast, confident the angle was right. One extra pass and I had a gap I couldn't fix. The chisel didn't make a mistake. I did. And sharp tools don't forgive sloppy thinking the way dull ones do.

I think about that in IT a lot.

When your team is good, when the systems are tuned and the access is wide, things move fast. A change gets pushed without a second set of eyes. A firewall rule gets added "just temporarily." An account stays active two weeks after someone leaves because nobody owns the offboarding process.

Sharp teams cut fast. That's mostly a good thing. But speed without a moment of pause is how small decisions become expensive ones.

I've learned this the hard way, usually right after something went sideways on a Friday afternoon.

The habit we built is simple. We explain every change in our Change Board before it runs. Having to talk about it out loud exposes more than you'd think, not because others see it, but because it makes you think about it differently.

What does your team do to slow down just enough before a sharp tool does exactly what you told it to?
